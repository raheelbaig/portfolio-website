# Cinematic Storyboard — "The Still Point"

**A short film in twelve scenes and a coda, experienced through scroll.**

Source of truth: the Product Vision. The constant is Raheel — still, lit, centered. The variable is the world, which assembles around him. Darkness is the stage; light is the storyteller. The visitor holds the camera: every scroll is a dolly move, every pause is a held frame.

The dramatic structure is a classic three-act arc:

- **Act I — Meeting (Scenes 1–4):** stillness, presence, and the person behind the work. Trust begins as curiosity.
- **Act II — Proof (Scenes 5–9):** the instruments, then the turn, then three built worlds. Trust becomes conviction. The projects are the climax.
- **Act III — Invitation (Scenes 10–12 + Coda):** what this means for the visitor, and a calm hand extended. Trust becomes action.

One law governs the whole film: **the spectacle happens once.** Everything before it practices restraint so that Scene 6 can spend the film's entire budget of wonder in a single moment — and everything after it returns to composure, now earned.

---

## ACT I — MEETING

---

### Scene 1 — "A Breath in the Dark" *(The Loader)*

**Purpose:** Set the emotional register before a single element of the site appears. This is the theater going dark before the projector starts.

**Visitor emotion:** Anticipation. The pleasant hush of something about to begin.

**What the visitor sees immediately:** Near-total darkness. A single thin line of light at the center — like the last light of a screen switching on, or the first — that breathes once: brightens, dims, brightens. No percentage counter, no spinner, no logo animation. At most, a whisper of text in a small technical voice: *"RAHEEL BAIG"* tracking slowly outward, letter-spacing widening like an inhale.

**Camera behavior:** The camera does not exist yet. This is the black leader at the head of the film reel. Total stillness.

**Scroll behavior:** Scroll is quietly ignored. The scene is over before impatience can form.

**Typography behavior:** One line only, small, uppercase, wide-tracked, precise. It does not animate in; it is simply *found* by the light, then released.

**Lighting direction:** A single sourceless glow at center frame, cool-neutral, at perhaps ten percent intensity. The darkness must feel velvety and intentional — a stage curtain, not an empty page.

**Ambient effects:** None. Absolute quiet is the effect. The absence teaches the visitor that on this site, everything visible is deliberate.

**Transition into next scene:** The line of light widens vertically — like an aperture opening, or elevator doors of pure light parting — and the darkness resolves into the hero scene already in progress. There is no cut; the loader *becomes* the film.

**Why this scene exists:** Every film needs a threshold between the world outside and the world of the story. Two seconds of composed darkness resets the visitor's pulse from "browsing" to "watching," and establishes the site's central promise: restraint first, so that light means something later.

**Maximum duration:** 1.5–2 seconds, hard limit. If loading takes longer, the scene holds its breath gracefully; it never adds more animation to fill time.

---

### Scene 2 — "The Still Point" *(Hero Introduction)*

**Purpose:** Introduce the protagonist. One human being, one name, one sentence of intent.

**Visitor emotion:** Arrest. The feeling of making unexpected eye contact with someone composed.

**What the visitor sees immediately:** Raheel's portrait, centered in darkness, lit like a film still — one key light finding the face and shoulder, the cutout edges dissolving into black so he seems to *inhabit* the dark rather than be pasted on it. His name arrives in monumental type, partly behind, partly in front of him, so man and name occupy the same physical space. Below, quieter: *Frontend Engineer.* A beat later, like a second line of dialogue: *Building modern web experiences powered by AI.* In a corner, in the small technical voice: a hint to scroll — a single downward line, slowly drawn.

**Camera behavior:** The film opens on a slow, almost imperceptible push-in toward the portrait — the classic opening dolly of a character study. It moves perhaps two percent over several seconds. The visitor shouldn't notice the movement, only feel that the frame is alive.

**Scroll behavior:** The first scroll is the handshake and must feel weighted and smooth, like pulling a heavy curtain that glides on perfect rails. Scrolling eases the name upward with parallax depth — the type moves at a different rate than the portrait, proving the space is three-dimensional. The portrait holds its ground longer than everything else. He is the still point; the first scroll demonstrates it.

**Typography behavior:** The name is the largest type in the entire film — editorial, confident, set with the care of a movie title. It enters with weight: rising a few pixels into place as if settling under its own gravity, no bounce, no letter-by-letter theatrics. The subtitle arrives in a calm cross-fade, one beat later.

**Lighting direction:** Single key light, high and slightly to one side — portrait-studio classic, Rembrandt-adjacent. A faint rim light separates his other shoulder from the dark. The rest of the frame stays black. Nothing else in the scene deserves light yet.

**Ambient effects:** The living portrait, at its subtlest: the key light shifts a few degrees in response to the visitor's cursor or device tilt, as if the visitor's attention were itself a light source. If the visitor is completely idle, the scene breathes — light drifting almost imperceptibly, a film still that hasn't quite frozen.

**Transition into next scene:** As the visitor scrolls, the monumental name exits upward and the camera appears to drift *around* the portrait a few degrees — the first hint of orbit, a promise quietly made and not yet kept. The darkness beyond his shoulder becomes the next scene's canvas.

**Why this scene exists:** Every audience decides in seconds whether a film has authority. This scene answers who, what, and *how it will feel* in one frame, and stakes the entire concept: the person is the constant. It also front-loads the two facts a skimming hiring manager needs before any patience is spent.

**Maximum duration:** 5–8 seconds of holding before the scroll hint gently pulses once. The scene is complete at a glance; everything after the first second is atmosphere, not information.

---

### Scene 3 — "Closer" *(About Me)*

**Purpose:** Convert the figure into a person. Voice, warmth, point of view — the first spoken lines of the character.

**Visitor emotion:** Familiarity beginning. The shift from "looking at" to "listening to."

**What the visitor sees immediately:** The portrait now off-center — shifted to one third of the frame, closer, more intimate framing — while the freed space fills with a short first-person monologue in large, readable lines. Not a biography. Three or four sentences with the cadence of voiceover: who he is, what he believes about the web, why he builds. Beneath, one quiet line of fact: five years, and where he's based.

**Camera behavior:** A slow lateral track — the camera stepping sideways and closer, the way a documentary reframes from formal portrait to conversation. The move happens *through* the visitor's scroll, so the visitor performs the reframing themselves.

**Scroll behavior:** Standard smooth scroll, but the monologue reveals line by line as the visitor descends — each sentence surfacing from ninety-percent darkness into full light as it reaches the reading zone, then politely dimming as it passes. The visitor reads at the pace of light.

**Typography behavior:** The monologue is set in the editorial voice at a middle scale — larger than body text, far smaller than the name — with generous leading. Lines brighten rather than move; the discipline here is that in this scene, *type does not travel, it only illuminates.*

**Lighting direction:** The key light softens and warms very slightly — two hundred kelvin toward candlelight, no more. The lighting difference between Scene 2 and Scene 3 is the difference between meeting someone on stage and talking with them after the show.

**Ambient effects:** Almost none. Perhaps the faintest suggestion of atmosphere in the black — a barely-visible volumetric softness around the light, like a quiet room with one lamp.

**Transition into next scene:** The final line of the monologue ends on intent — something like *"Five years of building. Here's the road."* On the next scroll, the portrait recedes slightly and a thin horizontal line of light draws itself across the darkness behind him: the timeline being born. The monologue's last light literally becomes the first light of the career journey.

**Why this scene exists:** Trust requires a voice, not just a face. Placing warmth immediately after the composed opening creates the film's essential contrast — cinema in the atmosphere, humanity in the words — and gives every following claim a person to belong to.

**Maximum duration:** 12–15 seconds of reading. The monologue must fit within four short sentences; if it needs a scrollbar's patience, it's a biography and gets cut.

---

### Scene 4 — "The Long Road" *(Career Journey)*

**Purpose:** Establish depth of experience — that the composure on display was earned across years, not styled overnight.

**Visitor emotion:** Respect. The quiet reassessment an audience makes when a character's backstory lands.

**What the visitor sees immediately:** A single line of light running horizontally through the dark — a road at night — with the portrait now small and distant at its far end, as if standing at the end of the path looking back. Along the line, a few luminous waypoints. Nothing is labeled until the visitor travels to it.

**Camera behavior:** A tracking shot along the road — the camera gliding parallel to the timeline the way a director shoots a character walking at dusk. The portrait at the end of the line very slowly grows as the visitor progresses: the journey literally leads to him.

**Scroll behavior:** This is the film's first *lateral* movement: vertical scroll translates into horizontal travel along the road. The change of axis is gentle and obvious within half a second, and it exists for a narrative reason — time moves sideways; only the present moves down. Each waypoint eases into focus as it reaches center frame: a year, a role, a single sentence of what changed.

**Typography behavior:** The technical voice leads here — years and roles in precise, small, engineered type, tabular and exact — while each waypoint carries one editorial line of meaning. Dates behave like instrumentation; meaning behaves like dialogue.

**Lighting direction:** Each waypoint is a small pool of light on the road, slightly warmer as they approach the present — the past cooler and dimmer, the recent years brighter, so the timeline itself performs a slow sunrise.

**Ambient effects:** The subtlest parallax in the black behind the road — two barely-visible depth layers drifting at different rates, giving the night actual distance without a single decorative particle.

**Transition into next scene:** The final waypoint is *now*. When the visitor reaches it, the road's light flows forward past the present marker and fans out — one line becoming several faint lines, like a river delta — and these lines carry the eye downward as the scroll axis returns to vertical. The paths ahead are plural because what comes next is capability, not chronology.

**Why this scene exists:** "5+ years" is a claim; a traveled road is an experience. This scene converts a résumé fact into felt history, and its cooler, dimmer palette makes it the film's quietest act — deliberately, because the brightest moment is now two scenes away and the contrast is being banked.

**Maximum duration:** 15–20 seconds at a reading pace, but skimmable in 6 — the waypoints must communicate shape (steady growth) even to a visitor who reads none of them.

## ACT II — PROOF

---

### Scene 5 — "The Instruments" *(Skills)*

**Purpose:** Lay out the tools — precisely, briefly, without worship. A craftsman opening the case, not a shop displaying inventory.

**Visitor emotion:** Confidence in the craftsman. The satisfaction of seeing well-kept tools.

**What the visitor sees immediately:** The delta of light-lines from the previous transition resolves into a calm, exact constellation: skill names set in the technical voice, arranged in a considered geometry — grouped by nature (interface, state and data, foundation, intelligence) with the groupings implied by proximity and light rather than boxes and labels. React, Next.js, TypeScript and their kin sit like labeled instruments on a dark bench. Off to one side, small: the portrait's hands would be here if the film were literal — instead, one editorial line: *"The instruments. The work is what they made."*

**Camera behavior:** A slow overhead drift — the camera looking down at the bench and gliding across it once, evenly, granting each group a moment of center frame without stopping on any.

**Scroll behavior:** Ordinary, smooth, unremarkable — deliberately the most conventional scroll in the film. Skills brighten softly as they pass the center of attention and dim as they leave. Nothing flies, counts up, or fills a percentage bar. The restraint *is* the statement.

**Typography behavior:** Monospaced-adjacent technical setting, identical size for every skill — no hierarchy games, no inflated hero technologies. Equality of type is a quiet claim of fluency across the board. The single editorial line is the only contrast.

**Lighting direction:** Even, cool, shadowless worklight across the whole bench — the first scene lit for clarity rather than drama. The absence of theatrical lighting here makes the visitor trust that earlier drama was a choice.

**Ambient effects:** One only: the group nearest the visitor's attention lifts in brightness by a breath, and the "intelligence" group — AI integration — carries the faintest slow pulse, one glow every few seconds, foreshadowing Scene 11.

**Transition into next scene:** This transition *is* Scene 6. As the visitor scrolls past the last instruments, every skill-light on the bench begins to drift from its position — slowly at first, like iron filings sensing a magnet — converging toward a point below. The film has been saving up for this.

**Why this scene exists:** By now the visitor has *felt* craft for five scenes; this is the receipt. Placing the stack immediately before the projects means every tool named here is proven seconds later — the list becomes a cast of characters introduced just before they perform.

**Maximum duration:** 8–10 seconds. A list is a list; dignity comes from brevity.

---

### Scene 6 — "The Turn" *(The One Earned Spectacle — Gateway to the Work)*

**Purpose:** Spend the film's entire budget of wonder in one moment: the world assembling around the builder. This is the thesis of the whole site performed literally, once.

**Visitor emotion:** Awe — the specific, grinning awe of watching something difficult look inevitable.

**What the visitor sees immediately:** The drifting skill-lights converge into darkness and, for half a second, the frame is as empty as the loader — a deliberate echo. Then the portrait fades in at center, and the assembled light *erupts outward around him* into structure: fragments of interface, panes of glass-like surface, threads of light connecting into architecture — an entire world constructing itself around the still figure in one continuous, orchestral movement. He does not move at all. Everything else does. At the peak of assembly, three luminous doorways resolve out of the structure, each carrying a name: **SalHub. StrangerUs. ZH.com.sg.**

**Camera behavior:** The promise from Scene 2 is finally kept: a full, slow orbital move — the camera circling the portrait perhaps a quarter-turn as the world builds, the only orbit in the film. It is the shot the entire storyboard exists to earn.

**Scroll behavior:** The visitor's scroll drives the assembly directly — construction advances only as they scroll, so the spectacle is *performed by the visitor*, not played at them. Scrolling backward disassembles it in reverse, gracefully. The scene occupies a longer scroll distance than any other, giving the moment room without ever taking control away.

**Typography behavior:** Almost none — one editorial line materializing mid-assembly, the film's thesis spoken aloud at last: *"I build the world around the work."* (Final copy may vary; the line must be short enough to read mid-motion.) The three project names arrive last, in the monumental register, small at this distance.

**Lighting direction:** The film's full dynamic range, used once: from near-black to the brightest frame of the experience at peak assembly, then settling to a rich, inhabited glow. The portrait's key light never wavers throughout — the world blazes; he stays evenly lit. Constancy under spectacle is the character note.

**Ambient effects:** Everything previously rationed appears here in concert — depth, parallax, volumetric light, motion — and nowhere else at this intensity, before or after.

**Transition into next scene:** The camera glides toward the first doorway — SalHub — and the assembled world's light narrows into that doorway's own palette. Entering the first project is simply the completion of the orbit's momentum. No cut; the spectacle *delivers* the visitor to the climax.

**Why this scene exists:** Awards and memories are made by one image. This is the image. Its placement is structural: it is the hinge between identity and proof, and because six scenes of restraint preceded it, it reads as revelation rather than decoration. It also reframes the projects that follow — they are not cards in a grid; they are worlds behind doors.

**Maximum duration:** 6–9 seconds of scroll-driven assembly. Long enough to be an event, short enough that no one checks the time. It must never play automatically for an idle visitor; unearned, it is a screensaver.

---

### Scenes 7–9 — "Three Worlds" *(Featured Projects — The Climax)*

The three projects share one scene grammar so the trilogy reads as range within mastery, not repetition. Described once, with each world's distinct character noted.

**Purpose:** Deliver proof. Each scene stages one built product as a world with its own weather, and tells its story in three beats: tension, decision, resolution.

**Visitor emotion:** Conviction, compounding. Scene 7: *"He can build."* Scene 8: *"He can build differently."* Scene 9: *"He can build for anyone."*

**What the visitor sees immediately:** Full-frame immersion in the world's own atmosphere — the project's interface presented large and alive, like a location establishing shot, with the project name in the monumental register and one editorial line naming the tension it answered. The portrait does not appear inside the worlds; the builder is felt through the work. (His absence here is deliberate — the only scenes without him — which quietly makes the point that shipped work stands on its own.)

**Camera behavior:** Each world opens on a slow establishing push across the product, then two or three composed close-ups — details of the interface framed like macro shots of a watch movement. The camera moves as if it *respects* the product: no swooping, only deliberate reveals.

**Scroll behavior:** Within each world, scroll advances the three story beats — tension, the interesting decision, the shipped result — with the product imagery reframing at each beat. Between beats, brief moments of visual rest. A visitor can pass through a world in three scrolls or linger; an unobtrusive threshold offers the deeper cut — full case study — for those who want the director's commentary.

**Typography behavior:** Three registers in strict roles: monumental for the world's name, editorial for the three story lines, technical for the quiet facts (role, stack used, outcome). The story lines appear one per beat — never stacked — so each world speaks exactly three sentences aloud.

**Lighting direction — where the trilogy differentiates:**
- **World One, SalHub:** confident daylight-neutral — clean, bright-on-dark clarity. The register of trust and utility.
- **World Two, StrangerUs:** the warmest scene in the film — human-hour tones, dusk warmth. The register of connection and people.
- **World Three, ZH.com.sg:** cool, exact, international — the crispest, most engineered light. The register of professional polish for a real-world client.

The visitor should feel the temperature change at each threshold, proving range through light before a word is read.

**Ambient effects:** Each world allows itself exactly one signature ambient behavior native to its character — gentle interface liveliness in the first, a soft warmth of presence in the second, precise micro-motion in the third. One per world, subtle, never simultaneous with text reading moments.

**Transition between worlds and onward:** Leaving a world, its light gathers back into a doorway that recedes, and the next doorway approaches along the residual curve of Scene 6's orbit — the visitor is walking a gallery of worlds, and the walk itself was built in the spectacle. After the third world, all three doorways appear together once, small and behind, like closing a triptych; ahead, a single calm horizon line of light forms — the bridge to Act III.

**Why these scenes exist:** They are the reason everything else exists. The vision names the projects the strongest proof; the storyboard therefore gives them the climax position, the most immersive framing, the only full-frame scenes, and the emotional peak — conviction — that the invitation will harvest.

**Maximum duration:** 10–15 seconds per world at reading pace; essence-in-one-viewport for skimmers. Three worlds together must never exceed the feeling of one movement of music — if fatigue is possible, a beat gets cut, not compressed.

## ACT III — INVITATION

---

### Scene 10 — "What We Could Build" *(Services)*

**Purpose:** Turn the film outward for the first time: from *what he has built* to *what he can build for you.* The pronoun of the site changes here.

**Visitor emotion:** Imagination. The visitor begins mentally casting their own project into what they've just seen.

**What the visitor sees immediately:** The calm horizon line resolves into a quiet, spacious scene: three to four offerings — the shape of engagements, not a menu of tasks — each stated in one editorial line with two lines of technical-voice specifics beneath. The portrait returns at the edge of frame, small, present again after the worlds: the builder stepping back into the room now that the work has spoken.

**Camera behavior:** A settled, level medium shot — tripod steadiness after the handheld heart of the film. The stillness of the frame communicates: the performance is over; this is conversation.

**Scroll behavior:** Simple vertical reveal, offerings surfacing into light one at a time, same grammar as the About monologue — a deliberate callback to the last time the site spoke person-to-person.

**Typography behavior:** Editorial lines lead each offering; the technical voice handles specifics. Type is now mid-scale and calm — the monumental register is finished until the farewell.

**Lighting direction:** Balanced and warm-neutral, the most evenly comfortable light so far — the lighting of a well-designed studio during a good meeting.

**Ambient effects:** Nearly none. The offering under the visitor's attention lifts a breath in brightness. The film is exhaling.

**Transition into next scene:** Beneath the final offering, a small point of light detaches and drifts down-frame ahead of the visitor's scroll — attentive, slightly alive, clearly *aware* of them. It is the first element in the entire film that moves toward the visitor. It leads into Scene 11.

**Why this scene exists:** Conviction without direction dissipates. This scene converts the climax's emotional capital into concrete possibility, and its deliberate plainness signals honesty — services rendered in the film's calmest voice read as confidence, not sales.

**Maximum duration:** 8–12 seconds. Offerings must be countable on one hand and readable in one breath each.

---

### Scene 11 — "The Attendant" *(AI Portfolio Assistant)*

**Purpose:** Demonstrate the subtitle — *powered by AI* — as a lived experience: a small intelligence, native to this world, offered as a courtesy.

**Visitor emotion:** Intrigue, then charm. The specific pleasure of being anticipated politely.

**What the visitor sees immediately:** The drifting point of light settles into a small, softly-lit presence — not a chat window slammed over the film, but a made-of-this-world object: a glowing form in the site's own light-language, with one line beside it in the editorial voice: *"Ask me anything about Raheel's work."* Beneath, two or three suggested questions in the technical voice — the kind a hiring manager actually has. It waits. It does not bounce, badge, or beg.

**Camera behavior:** A gentle rack focus: the scene's background softens as the attendant sharpens — the film's way of saying *you may speak now* without raising its voice.

**Scroll behavior:** Scrolling past is completely frictionless — the attendant yields gracefully and follows at the frame's edge thereafter, small and quiet, available for the rest of the film. Engaging it holds the scene in a soft focus state; leaving the exchange returns to the film without a seam.

**Typography behavior:** The attendant speaks only in the technical voice — precise, brief, a little dry — because it is an instrument, not a second protagonist. The visitor's own words appear in the editorial register: the guest is given the better font.

**Lighting direction:** The attendant carries its own small warm-cool light, the same faint pulse foreshadowed on the intelligence group in Scene 5 — a promise planted six scenes ago, kept here.

**Ambient effects:** The pulse, and nothing else. Its restraint is what separates an attendant from a gimmick.

**Transition into next scene:** Whether or not the visitor engages, the scroll beyond dims the studio light gradually — the film beginning its final approach to the register it opened with. The attendant's glow remains at frame's edge, a lantern accompanying the visitor into the last scenes.

**Why this scene exists:** Every claim on this site is demonstrated before it is listed — except, until now, the boldest one in the subtitle. This scene closes that loop: AI presented as attentiveness and courtesy, in-world and optional, converting the site's most buzzword-prone promise into its most polite feature.

**Maximum duration:** 5 seconds to understand and pass, unlimited to engage. The scene must cost a hurried visitor nothing.

---

### Scene 12 — "The Invitation" *(Contact)*

**Purpose:** Ask. Once, warmly, with total composure.

**Visitor emotion:** Ease. The decision to reach out should feel like agreeing with a sentence they were already finishing.

**What the visitor sees immediately:** The film returns home: darkness, the portrait centered and lit exactly as in Scene 2 — but the visitor now knows this room. The framing is a degree closer, the light a degree warmer: the same person, after the story. One editorial line — *"Let's build something worth the light."* (or final equivalent) — and beneath it a single luminous action: **Let's Connect.** To the side, small, in the technical voice: the email address written out plainly, and the socials. No form. No fields. One ask.

**Camera behavior:** The mirror of the opening: a slow push-in, the film's first camera move repeated as its last — but this time it settles slightly closer than Scene 2 ever came. The entire journey, measured in one increment of intimacy.

**Scroll behavior:** The scroll comes to a natural, cushioned rest here — the felt bottom of the story. The action glows faintly under attention; pressing it is the cut to black.

**Typography behavior:** The final line is set in the editorial voice at generous scale — the last spoken dialogue. **Let's Connect** is treated with the same typographic dignity as the name was in Scene 2: the film opened on who he is and closes on what you and he might do.

**Lighting direction:** Scene 2's key light, warmed by everything between — the visual proof that the visitor and the site have history now. The brightest object in the frame is the invitation itself: for the first and only time, the light's focal point is an action.

**Ambient effects:** The idle breath from Scene 2 returns, slightly slower. The attendant's lantern rests quietly at the edge, in case a final question stands between the visitor and hello.

**Transition into next scene:** A gentle settling downward into the coda — the credits after the final frame.

**Why this scene exists:** Endings are what audiences carry out of the theater. By mirroring the opening, the film gives its ask the weight of a completed arc — the visitor isn't submitting a form at the bottom of a page; they are answering a person they've just spent three minutes meeting.

**Maximum duration:** This scene has no clock. It is where the film is content to hold forever.

---

### Coda — "After the Credits" *(Footer)*

**Purpose:** Close the file with the same care it opened. Credits, not clutter.

**Visitor emotion:** Completeness. The small satisfaction of a film that ends properly.

**What the visitor sees immediately:** A slim, dark band in the technical voice: name, year, the quiet essentials — socials, email once more, a legal whisper if needed — and one final line of dry, confident character: *"Designed and built by Raheel Baig. Obviously."* (tone adjustable; the wink is the point). Set exactly, aligned perfectly: the last thing juries and peers inspect, kept flawless.

**Camera behavior / Scroll / Lighting / Ambient:** Static, minimal, evenly dim, none. The projector light cooling.

**Transition:** None. Films end.

**Why this scene exists:** The invisible-places principle from the vision: excellence where no one is forced to look is the most credible signal of all. A composed footer is the site's firm handshake goodbye.

**Maximum duration:** Two seconds of glance. Anything a footer needs longer for belongs in a scene.

---

## The Journey in One Breath

Darkness breathes → a person appears → he speaks → the road he traveled → the tools he carries → **the world assembles around him, once** → three worlds entered and believed → what he could build for you → a small intelligence offers help → the room from the beginning, warmer → *Let's Connect* → credits.

Restraint for six scenes buys one act of wonder; wonder buys conviction through three worlds; conviction is asked for exactly once, in the calmest voice in the film.
